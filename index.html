Chapter 1

Scientific Study

6 Steps of Scientific Learning

Scientists solve problems using these 6 simple steps in order:

  • Observation: Noticing a problem or seeing something interesting in nature.
  • Hypothesis: Making a smart, logical guess about the answer.
  • Experiment: Testing your guess by doing practical experiments.
  • Result & Conclusion: Checking the data and writing down what happened.
  • Hypothesis Testing: Finding out if your initial guess was correct or wrong.
  • Reporting: Sharing your final results with others.

Scientific Notation (Powers of 10)

Used to write very big numbers or very small numbers easily:

Number = m × 10n
(The first number 'm' must always be between 1 and 9)
  • Big Numbers (Positive Power): Move the decimal point to the left.
    Example: 150,000,000 km becomes 1.5 × 108 km.
  • Small Numbers (Negative Power): Move the decimal point to the right.
    Example: 0.0000001 m becomes 1.0 × 10-7 m.

Least Count

The Least Count is the smallest value that an instrument can measure accurately.

  • Normal Scale (Ruler): Can measure down to 0.1 cm (or 1 mm).
  • Digital Stopwatch: Can measure down to 0.01 seconds.

Exam Secret for A+

Question: Why do we repeat experiments many times and take the average?
Answer: To avoid simple personal mistakes and get the most accurate result.

Chapter 2

Classification of Living Beings

What is Classification?

Grouping living things into different categories based on how similar they are. It helps us study them easily.

Scientific Names (Binomial Nomenclature)

Every living thing has a unique scientific name made of two words (started by Carolus Linnaeus):

  • First word: Genus name (Always starts with a Capital letter).
  • Second word: Species name (Starts with a small letter).
  • Rule: Underline both words separately when writing by hand (e.g., Homo sapiens).

The 7 Levels of Classification

Living things are ranked from top to bottom:
Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

The Five Kingdoms (Robert Whittaker)

  • 1. Monera: Tiny, single-celled organisms with no proper cell nucleus. (Example: Bacteria).
  • 2. Protista: Single-celled organisms with a proper nucleus. (Example: Amoeba).
  • 3. Fungi: Organisms that cannot make their own food and grow on dead matter. (Example: Mushroom, Yeast).
  • 4. Plantae: Green plants that make food using sunlight. (Example: Fern, Mango tree).
  • 5. Animalia: Animals that eat other things and can move. (Example: Fish, Human).
Chapter 3

Mushroom

Parts of a Mushroom

Mushrooms are fungi that grow on wet, dead leaves or wood. They have two main parts:

  • Mycelium: The hidden root-like structure under the ground made of tiny threads called hyphae. They absorb food from dead matter.
  • Fruiting Body: The visible part above the ground. It has a stalk (stipe) and an umbrella-shaped cap (pileus). Under the cap, there are thin plates called gills.

Life Cycle & Reproduction

Mushrooms reproduce by creating tiny dust-like units called spores. Inside the gills, club-shaped cells called basidia grow exactly 4 haploid basidiospores. These spores float away in the wind to grow new mushrooms in dark, moist places.

Edible vs. Poisonous Mushrooms

Edible (Safe to Eat): White Button mushroom (Agaricus bisporus). They are rich in healthy vitamins, proteins, and minerals.

Poisonous (Danger!): Wild mushrooms with bright colors (like red caps), a ring on the stalk, or a cup-like base (volva). Never eat wild mushrooms as they can cause death.

Chapter 4

Evolution

What is Evolution?

The very slow and continuous process by which simple ancient living things changed into complex modern living things over millions of years.

Evidences (Proofs) of Evolution

  • 1. Fossils: Remains of dead ancient plants and animals trapped inside rocks. Older, simpler fossils are found in deep rock layers; newer, complex ones are on top.
  • 2. Homologous Organs: Organs that look similar inside and share a common ancestor, but do different jobs now. (Example: Human arm, Bat wing, Horse front leg).
  • 3. Analogous Organs: Organs that look different inside but do the exact same job. (Example: Wing of a butterfly and wing of a bird).
  • 4. Vestigial Organs: Useless, tiny body parts left over from our ancestors. (Example: Appendix and wisdom teeth in humans).

Main Theories of Evolution

Theory Name Scientist Main Idea
Lamarckism Jean Lamarck Body parts used more become larger; parts not used disappear. These changes pass to children.
Darwinism Charles Darwin Living things fight for food and space. Nature selects the strongest or fittest to survive (Natural Selection).
Chapter 5

Body Structure and Life Process

Tissues (Groups of Cells)

A tissue is a group of similar cells working together to perform a single job.

  • Plant Tissues: Meristematic (growing tissues found at root/shoot tips) and Permanent (like Xylem which carries water, and Phloem which carries food).
  • Animal Tissues: Epithelial (skin protection), Muscular (movement), Nervous (signals), and Connective (Blood and Bone).

The Human Brain

  • Cerebrum: The biggest part. Controls thinking, memory, speech, learning, and senses.
  • Cerebellum: Found at the back. Controls body balance, walking, and running.
  • Medulla Oblongata: The brain stem. Controls automatic actions like heartbeat, breathing, and swallowing.

Chapter 6

Nature and Environment

What is an Ecosystem?

A balance system where living things (**Biotic factors**) interact with non-living things (**Abiotic factors** like air, water, soil, and sunlight).

Living Groups (Biotic Components)

  • Producers: Green plants that make their own food using sunlight.
  • Consumers: Animals that eat plants or other animals. (Herbivores eat plants; Carnivores eat meat).
  • Decomposers: Tiny bacteria and fungi that clean the earth by eating dead plants and animals and recycling nutrients back into the soil.

Food Chains and Food Webs

  • Food Chain: A simple linear line showing who eats whom.
    Example: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Hawk.
  • Food Web: Many food chains joined together. It shows how animals have multiple choices for food in real life.

Types of Interactions (Relationships)

  • Mutualism (+/+): Both benefit. (Example: Bee and Flower—bee gets food, flower gets pollinated).
  • Commensalism (+/0): One benefits, the other is unhurt. (Example: A spider building a web in a tree).
  • Parasitism (+/-): One benefits, the other is harmed. (Example: Mosquitoes sucking human blood).
